Missing Data Report

1. Missing Value Summary:
After checking the dataset, I found that around 96 percent 
of the data had missing values across several columns, most 
commonly in temperature and heart_rate. The total number of 
missing values was approximately 323, which is not negligible 
but small enough to handle with standard imputation methods.

2. Missing Data Patterns:
Most of the missing values occurred because we forced up 
sampling from monthly to daily meaning that we are producing 
a lot of missing values. It makes sense that 96 percent of the 
data is missing because we upsampled from month to day.

3. Imputation Method:
I used time based interpolation (`method='time'`) to fill the missing values. 
This approach estimates missing points by looking at surrounding timestamps and 
interpolating based on actual time differences. For a few variables where interpolation 
didnt make sense (like categorical flags), I used forward fill (`ffill`) to carry the
last valid value forward.

4. Rationale:
I chose time based interpolation because the data is time series with 
uneven sampling intervals. Linear or mean imputation would ignore time 
gaps and could distort the trends. Time interpolation, on the other hand, 
preserves continuity and gives smoother transitions between recorded points.

5. Pros and Cons:
The main advantage of this approach is that it maintains realistic trends over 
time and avoids sudden jumps that could come from other methods. However, 
it assumes that changes between two observations are gradual, which might not always 
be true, especially in ICU data where vital signs can change abruptly.

6. Example:
Before interpolation:
    2025-11-10  98.4
    2025-11-11   NaN
    2025-11-12  99.2

After interpolation:
    2025-11-10  98.4
    2025-11-11  98.8
    2025-11-12  99.2

This confirms that the interpolation filled missing values reasonably without introducing unrealistic spikes or trends.
